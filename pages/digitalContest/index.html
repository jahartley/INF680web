RTTY Contesting

The first RTTY contest was sponsored by the RTTY Society of Southern California in 1957. Because of the need to get as many two way contacts (QSO’s) as possible during a contest, the minimum amount of information that was needed to be passed between the stations became the norm, with exchanges reduced to:

CQ K4AHZ EM18 (CQ means calling all stations, and K4AHZ is the callsign of the station calling out, and the location of the station using the Maidenhead Grid Square system.)

Another operator responds with:
K4AHZ W9XYZ EN37 (my callsign, their call sign, and their grid square)

Then signal reports which also indicate that two way communication is established by acknowledging the callsign of the responding station, with a signal strength report:
W9XYZ K4AHZ -13

And W9XYZ responds
K4AHZ W9XYZ R-11 (the R before the signal report from W9XYZ means they acknowledge the signal report)

And sometimes a final:
K4AHZ W9XYZ RR73 (“73” is a common HAM signoff meaning Best Regards.)

Frequency vs transmission distance

A quick note on Frequency vs transmission distance. The lower frequency used usually equates to increased transmission distances as lower frequency waves penetrate solid materials better, but due to the lower frequency have reduced data transmission rates.

In the U.S., General and Amateur Extra licensed HAM operators commonly use the 160 meter and 80 meter bands at 1.8 MHz and 3.5 MHz respectively, which can often reach the other side of the earth through various transmission modes. Higher frequencies like the 70 cm, 33 cm, and 23 cm bands are very line of sight and will be blocked by a few walls in your house. This is the reason you have better 2.4 GHz WIFI reception at your house than with 5 GHz WIFI, but at a reduced potential data rate. The same is true with the usage of lower frequency cell phone radio bands, lower speeds but better connections when there is no line of sight to the cell phone tower antenna.

Amateur Radio common digital modes

A large number of digital data modes have been used on HAM radio frequencies to include:
RTTY using Frequency Shift Keying
AX.25 Packet radio using Bell 103 Audio Frequency Shift Keying modulation at 300 bits per second, with a derived version of the X.25 Data link protocol suite, which has been modified for radio use, and includes the physical, data link and network layers of the OSI model. AX.25 also allows Internet Protocol network layer to be used for sending any IP packets… extremely slowly at 300 bits per second, but potentially over vast distances like to send an email from a small sailboat in the middle of the Pacific ocean.
A few Phase shift keying modes like:
PSK31, a 31-baud binary phase shift keying mode
QPSK31, a 31-baud quadrature phase shift keying mode
And Multiple frequency shift keying (MFSK) modes:
FSK441, JT65, Olivia, and FT8 and JS8Call.

FT8 is slightly different in that most of these modes are designed to pass any sort of data you would like to send, sometimes even photos or slow scan video. FT8 is designed specifically for contesting, with many special features specifically to meet the needs of weak signal contest reception.
